Who do I Think I am Anyway?

Hi! I'm Miguel. And I love computer science (when I'm not getting NullPointerExceptions). I strive for creative, elegant, and remarkable design in my work, while maintaining a healthy balance with efficiency.

I went to South River High School in Edgewater, MD, where I built my knowledge base for STEM fields under Project Lead the Way, a program that guided me to graduation with varied fields of Engineering courses under my belt. Looking back, these courses and my demanding teachers instilled in me the virtues of patience and attention to detail that are now so imperative in the field of computer science.

I transferred to the University of Maryland intending to become an engineer. However, one thing led to another, and I eventually realized my passion for computer science. I am now enjoying new projects like developing Android applications, designing and implementing a database, and this website, which I designed mainly with HTML and CSS. I am graduating on May 2018 and I am currently a Corporate Technical Intern for an IT solutions company for the Spring 2017 semester.

COURSES

CMSC 131 & 132 - Object Oriented Programing

with Professors Evan Golub & Tom Reinhardt

Emphasizes understanding and implementation of applications using object-oriented techniques. Develops skills such as program design and testing as well as implementation of programs using a graphical IDE. Design, build, test, and debug medium -size software systems and learn to use relevant tools. Use object-oriented methods to create effective and efficient problem solutions. Use and implement application programming interfaces (APIs). Programming done in Java.

TL;DR: I learned Java.
If you're not familiar with computer science, Java is one of the most common programming languages companies look for.

CMSC 216 - INTRODUCTION TO COMPUTER SYSTEMS

with Professor Nelson Padua-Perez

Machine representation of data including integers and floating point. Modern computer architectural features and their interaction with software (registers, caches). Interaction between user programs and the OS: system class, process, and thread management. Optimizing software to improve runtime performance using both compilers and hand turning

CMSC 250 - Discrete Structures

with Professor Tom Reinhardt

Fundamental mathematical concepts related to computer science, including finite and infinite sets, relations, functions, and propositional logic. Introduction to other techniques, modeling and solving problems in computer science. Introduction to permutations, combinations, graphs, and trees with selected applications.

CMSC 330 - Organization of Programming Languages

with Dr. Anwar Mamat

The semantics of programming languages and their run-time organization. Several different models of languages are discussed, including procedural (e.g., C, Pascal), functional (e.g., ML, LISP), rule-based (e.g., Prolog), and object-oriented (e.g., C++, Smalltalk). Run-time structures, including dynamic versus static scope rules, storage for strings, arrays, records, and object inheritance are explored.

CMSC 351 - Algorithms

with Professor Clyde Kruskal

A systematic study of the complexity of some elementary algorithms related to sorting, graphs and trees, and combinatorics. Algorithms are analyzed using mathematical techniques to solve recurrences and summations.

CMSC 420 - Discrete Structures

with Dr. Michelle Hugue

Description, properties, and storage allocation of data structures including lists and trees. Algorithms for manipulating structures. Applications from areas such as data processing, information retrieval, symbol manipulation, and operating systems.

CMSC 436 - Programming Handheld Systems

with Professor Charles Song

Fundamental principles and concepts that underlie the programming of handheld systems, such as mobile phones, personal digital assistants, and tablet computers. Particular emphasis will be placed on concepts such as limited display size, power, memory and CPU speed; and new input modalities, where handheld systems differ substantially from non-handheld systems, and thus require special programming tools and approaches. Concepts and principles applied in the context of an existing handset programming platform.

BMGT 355 - FOUNDATIONS OF MARKETING

with Professor Mary Harms

Introduces the concepts and principles of marketing. Provides an overview of all the concepts in marketing including relationship marketing, product development, pricing, promotion, marketing research, consumer behavior, international marketing, distribution and internal marketing to employees.

Experience

Paradyme Management, Inc.

Working for Paradyme has been an absolute pleasure. As a Corporate Technical Intern, I get my first real taste of what it is like to be a professional. I've learned what its like to work with others in a company that's thriving, but I've also learned to work independently and develop my research skills when necessary.

My team is currently working on a Database project, and I'm learning all the basics of designing and implementing a database. Through this project, I am learning on the fly how to code in Python, and gaining experience using the web-framework Django. I have also become familiar with MySql and the Windows command prompt.

Being here has also taught me about the importance of company culture. Though the work is very demanding, everyone here is happy because of the kind of environment we are in. Paradyme is diverse and makes an effort to celebrate and observe differences in traditions. The CEO, Viraj Ghandi, takes the time to interact with us interns directly and show that he cares, and he always shows what it takes to not only start a business, but to make it successful and continue growing. From him, I am learning some of the techniques to becoming a successful businessman, especially in the tech industry.

Thompson Creek Window Company


My time at Thompson Creek Window Company really opened my eyes to the importance of marketing in business. As part of the marketing team, we encouraged and competed against each other through our marketing goals. Here, I learned an array of marketing and sales techniques.

I started this job off with no marketing or sales experience. But by the end of my tenure, I was competing with people who trained me. I had achieved a high of setting seven in-home customer appointments with a Thompson Creek foreman in a 4 hour shift. To put some perspective, an average day for anyone would be two.

Working here allowed my communication skills to improve significantly. I felt my confidence in myself and my ability to express myself swell up. But more importantly, I learned how to persevere. Any salesman knows that there will be awful days, but here I became more thick-skinned and willing to look past the bad days and focus on the present.


Checkers Drive-In

My first job was at Checkers as a regular crew member. I was part of the original crew that worked in the grand opening of the fast-food chain in Crofton, MD. The environment here was fast paced, and I had to learn to be efficient. In fact, I would get so overwhelmed that I made an algorithm of sorts to be as efficient as possible when doing my tasks.

I was first trained as a cashier, becoming customer service oriented, but eventually I became proficient in all stations of the restaurant, and became one of their most versatile employees. After a year, I had been promoted to "blue shirt" which meant that managers could relay responsibilities to me when they needed to step out. I became a leader in making sure the day-to-day operations went as smoothly as possible. By the end of my tenure, I was offered a full time managerial position.

Working here taught me some lessons that only people who have worked in fast-food can learn. For one, I learned there is so much more work involved in the job than the depiction of a bored employee in that TV show you watched recently seems to imply. Especially in slow days when less employees work and spikes in customers can be difficult to cope with. It is also humbling to work with people whose very lives rely on the same job that you can live without. No task is too small or too large if you value your job. I think my experience here allowed me to grow as a person and be more perceptive of the situations of people of different backgrounds.

GEMS

During my internship at GEMS I met a lot of wonderful people like me who didn't know what they wanted to do. Though I had an inclination for the tech industry, I wasn't sure exactly what I would be willing to do every day as an adult. GEMS was great because they taught me through various hands-on activities without getting too deep into each topic.

One project in particular was very memorable because I worked on making a character on a video game do simple tasks. I programmed it to move forwards, left, right, backwards, and also attack enemies. The attack was particularly complex because its success depends so much on position. Though this project didn't convince me to pursue computer science, I think that looking back, in some unconscious way, it played a role in my decision.
